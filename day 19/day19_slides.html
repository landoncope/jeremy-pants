Vocab Review üìö

Phishing

Quick Reminder: A type of online scam where criminals send fake emails, texts, or messages pretending to be from legitimate companies or people to trick you into giving away passwords, credit card numbers, or other personal information.

Think of it like: Someone wearing a fake UPS uniform knocking on your door asking for your house keys "for a delivery." That's what phishing does online - criminals dress up as trusted companies to steal your info!

üö® Red Flags to Watch For:
  • Spelling errors and bad grammar
  • Generic greetings ("Dear Customer")
  • Urgent threats ("Your account will be suspended!")
  • Requests for passwords or credit cards
  • Suspicious links or email addresses

Malware

Quick Reminder: Malicious software designed to damage, disrupt, or steal data from your computer, phone, or network. It's the umbrella term for all bad software including viruses, ransomware, spyware, and trojans.

Think of it like: Digital germs or pests! Just like how bacteria makes you sick or termites destroy a house, malware infects your device to steal info, spy on you, or hold your files hostage.

Types You Should Know:

  • ü¶† Virus: Spreads and copies itself
  • üîí Ransomware: Locks your files for money
  • üëÅÔ∏è Spyware: Watches and steals passwords
  • üê¥ Trojan: Pretends to be legit software

New Vocabulary üìö

Bug

Definition: An error, flaw, or fault in software or hardware that causes it to produce incorrect or unexpected results, or to behave in unintended ways.

Real-world: Like a typo in code that breaks your program! When your game crashes, a character walks through walls, or a calculator gives wrong answers - that's a bug. "Debugging" means finding and fixing these problems.

Common Types of Bugs:

  • üî¥ Syntax Error: Typos or wrong code grammar
  • üü° Logic Error: Code runs but does the wrong thing
  • üü† Runtime Error: Crashes while running (like dividing by zero)
  • üîµ Infinite Loop: Code gets stuck repeating forever

The First ACTUAL Bug! üêõ

On September 9, 1947, something incredible happened at Harvard University...

The Story: Engineers were working on the Harvard Mark II computer (a massive room-sized computer) when it malfunctioned. When they investigated, they found a moth trapped in one of the computer's mechanical relays! They carefully removed it and taped it into their logbook with the note: "First actual case of bug being found."

The original 1947 logbook with the moth taped to the page. Now at the Smithsonian!

üí° Important Context: The term "bug" was actually used by engineers before 1947 (Thomas Edison used it in the 1870s!), but this was the first time an ACTUAL insect bug caused a computer problem. The team was making a joke when they wrote "first actual case of bug being found"!

The Harvard Mark II Computer üñ•Ô∏è

What computers looked like in 1947...

About the Harvard Mark II:

  • üìè Size: Filled an entire room - about 40 feet long!
  • ‚ö° Technology: Used 13,000 electromechanical relays (physical switches that clicked)
  • üêå Speed: Could do about 3 calculations per second (your phone can do billions!)
  • üéñÔ∏è Purpose: Built for the U.S. Navy for ballistic calculations
  • üîä Sound: Super loud! All those relays clicking made it sound like a room full of knitting needles
  • üí° No Screen: Output was printed on paper or punched into cards - no monitors existed!
  • üë©‚Äçüíª Grace Hopper: Famous computer scientist who worked on it and popularized "bug" and "debug" as programmer language
ü§Ø Mind-Blowing: The Harvard Mark II filled an entire room and was less powerful than a calculator watch! Your smartphone is literally millions of times more powerful than this computer. We've come incredibly far in just 77 years!
üí° Why Relays? Before transistors and microchips were invented, computers used physical mechanical switches called relays. That's why a moth could get stuck in one - they were big enough for a bug to crawl into!
