Famous Hackers: The Legends

Black Hat ‚Üí White Hat

Kevin Mitnick - "The World's Most Wanted Hacker"

Started hacking as a teenager in 1981. His most famous trick? Social Engineering - he called Motorola, asked for the security manager's username and password... and they just gave it to him!

He was once on the FBI's Most Wanted list and spent 5 years in prison. After his release, he became a security consultant helping companies protect themselves - proof that hacking skills can lead to a legit career!

Lesson Learned: The biggest security weakness isn't technology - it's PEOPLE. Never give out passwords, even if someone sounds official!

More Hacker Stories

Grey Hat

Gary McKinnon - "The Biggest Military Hack Ever"

Between 2001-2002, McKinnon hacked into NASA and US military systems from his bedroom in the UK. He left messages like "Your security is crap." His reason? He was looking for evidence of UFOs! Caused $700,000 in damage.

Black Hat (15 years old!)

Michael Calce "MafiaBoy"

In February 2000, this 15-year-old Canadian took down Yahoo!, Amazon, CNN, eBay, and Dell - the biggest websites at the time. He did it just to impress his online friends. Cost companies about $1.7 billion.

Wild Fact: In Las Vegas, hackers broke into a casino's network through a FISH TANK! The smart thermometer in the tank was connected to the internet, giving hackers a way in.

The Three Types of Hackers

üé© Black Hat Hackers

ü§ç White Hat Hackers

ü©∂ Grey Hat Hackers

Top Web Vulnerabilities (OWASP Top 10)

These are the most common security problems in websites and apps:

#1 - Broken Access Control

When you can access stuff you shouldn't by just changing the URL. Example: changing ?user=123 to ?user=456 to see someone else's account.

#3 - Injection Attacks

When hackers type code into input fields (like login forms) and the website accidentally runs it. The most famous is SQL Injection - typing database commands into a username field!

#7 - Authentication Failures

Weak passwords, no two-factor authentication, session tokens that don't expire. The Colonial Pipeline hack in 2021 happened because of ONE compromised password with no 2FA!

Case Study: Colonial Pipeline (2021)

What Happened:

Hackers from the DarkSide group used a SINGLE stolen password to get into Colonial Pipeline's network. No two-factor authentication was required.

The Impact:

Colonial Pipeline supplies 45% of the East Coast's fuel. They had to shut down for 5 days, causing gas shortages and panic buying across multiple states.

The Ransom:

The company paid $4.4 MILLION in Bitcoin to the hackers. (The FBI later recovered about half of it.)

The Scary Part: All of this happened because of ONE weak password and no two-factor authentication. That's why your school makes you use complex passwords and 2FA!

The Biggest Data Breaches Ever

Yahoo (2013-2014)

3 BILLION accounts - Every single Yahoo account was compromised. Names, emails, passwords, security questions - all stolen. The largest breach in history.

Equifax (2017)

147 million people - Social Security numbers, birth dates, addresses stolen. The hackers got in through an unpatched vulnerability the company KNEW about but didn't fix. Cost: at least $575 million in settlements.

Vladimir Levin hacked Citibank and stole $10 million - the first major online bank robbery. He got caught and received only 3 years in prison.

Protect Yourself: The Basics

1. Use Strong, Unique Passwords

Use a password manager. Never reuse passwords. If one site gets hacked, hackers try that password on every other site!

2. Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Even if someone steals your password, they can't get in without the second factor. Use an authenticator app, not just SMS.

3. Be Suspicious of Emails & Links

Phishing is the #1 attack method. Check the sender's actual email address. When in doubt, go to the website directly instead of clicking links.

4. Keep Software Updated

Those annoying update notifications? They often fix security holes. The Equifax breach happened because they didn't update!

Spot the Phishing Attack!

Phishing is the #1 cybercrime according to the FBI. Can you spot the red flags?

Example Email:

From: security@amaz0n-support.com
Subject: URGENT: Your account will be suspended!!!

Dear Valued Costumer,

We have detected suspiscious activity on your acount. Click here immediatly to verify your information or your account will be permanantly locked!

Click Here to Verify NOW

Red Flags:
  • Fake domain (amaz0n with a zero, not amazon)
  • URGENT/threatening language
  • Spelling errors ("Costumer", "suspiscious", "immediatly")
  • Generic greeting ("Valued Customer")
  • Pressure to click immediately

Your Hacking Toolkit:

  • Application Tab - View LocalStorage, Cookies, Session Storage
  • Console Tab - Run JavaScript and see hints
  • Sources Tab - View the actual JavaScript code
  • Network Tab - See what data is being sent
